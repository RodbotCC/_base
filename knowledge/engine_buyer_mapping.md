# Engine 5: Buyer Mapping — The Map Step

**Thesis:** Discovery should not leave you with vibes. It should leave you with coordinates. Forced comparisons turn messy preferences into usable buyer data.

**Verb in the method:** Map. Locate the buyer's coordinates.

**Output:** Coordinates — a buyer vector across a few canonical axes.

## Atomic ideas

- Open-ended discovery creates sentiment but not structure.
- Either/or questions generate cleaner data than broad "what do you want?" prompts.
- Buyers place themselves more accurately than they describe themselves.
- Every forced comparison reveals preference, constraint, and tension.
- Three or four dichotomies produce a working buyer profile.
- Recommendations close better when they echo the buyer's own placement data.
- Great discovery builds a vector, not a vibe.

## The compression one-liner

**"People struggle to describe what they want from nothing, but they can place themselves between two things."**

## Best lesson angle

"Don't ask for a speech. Offer a contrast."

## The canonical dichotomies

Five axes that produce a working buyer vector in the events/catering instantiation — generalizable to any buyer:

1. **Scale Preference:** intimate / balanced / expansive
2. **Decision Style:** decisive / consensus / partner-led
3. **Control Preference:** hands-off / collaborative / high-control
4. **Budget Posture:** value-maximizing / balanced / premium-certainty
5. **Customization Appetite:** bespoke / guided / packaged

The buyer vector is just their position on these axes. Once you have it, the proposal can echo the vector back — which is why match-score matters. "The proposal should sound like the map talking back."

## The mechanism in specific

- **Guided Contrast** (was: Vanishing Dilemma): "Is this feeling more like an intimate experience, or more like a big shared celebration?"
- **Constraint Sorting** (was: Shotgunning): "When you say you're still comparing, are you mostly comparing price, reliability, food quality, or just overall trust?"

Both force location without forcing fabrication. The buyer isn't being asked to invent; they're being asked to place.

## Prose seed

Most discovery calls produce a cloud of adjectives: exciting, elegant, not too formal, somewhere in the middle. None of that closes. A strong discovery process does something much more useful. It forces the buyer to locate themselves. Intimate or expansive. Guided or custom. Certainty or economy. Once they start placing themselves between anchors, the fog breaks and the deal finally has geometry.

## Why this connects to the framework corpus

This is **the ratio lattice applied to a person.** See `dimensionless_ratio_lattice_and_dynamical_completeness`: a regime is specified by a small set of dimensionless ratios, not by a mass of absolute measurements. The buyer vector is exactly that move — five dimensionless positions (each between two poles) specify the buyer's regime. Everything downstream (proposal, framing, price posture) is that regime's dynamics.

The **anchors/comparators** doctrine is also load-bearing here. Each dichotomy names two anchors and asks the buyer to place themselves along the comparator between them. This is why the move is clean: you're not asking for an absolute value, you're asking for a dimensionless ratio.
