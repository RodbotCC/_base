# Engine 2: Tribal Alignment — The Align Step

**Thesis:** Surface rapport comes from shared tastes; durable rapport comes from shared standards and shared refusal. That is what creates tribe, and tribe is what protects margin.

**Verb in the method:** Align. Name the broken standard you both reject.

**Output:** Tribe — alliance-dynamic replaces buyer-seller dynamic.

## Atomic ideas

- Shared preferences create affinity; shared boundaries create identity.
- The most powerful bond in sales is mutual rejection of the same low standard.
- The right enemy is a broken norm, not a named competitor.
- Your process becomes persuasive when it is framed as a refusal of chaos.
- Buyers stop shopping as aggressively when they feel moral alignment, not just feature alignment.
- Price becomes harder to weaponize when the deal feels like tribal membership.
- Competitors can underbid a service; they cannot underbid a shared line in the sand.

## The compression one-liner

**"Nobody switches tribes for a 10% discount."**

## Best lesson angle

"Sell the line, not the like."

## Operational translation

Competitive insulation and margin defense. Tribal Alignment is why shared-enemy moves aren't just feel-good — they're the highest-margin moat in sales. Discounts compete on price; standards compete on identity. The buyer who has named their line in the sand *and heard you name it back* has stopped shopping.

## CRM / KPI language

- Non-Negotiable Standard Captured

## Prose seed

A client can like you and still shop you. They can enjoy the call, compliment your process, and still hand the business to someone five percent cheaper. The game changes when they realize you hate the same thing they hate. Not a competitor. A standard. The kind of sloppiness, opacity, or indifference they have already paid for once and refuse to pay for again. That's when you stop sounding like a vendor and start sounding like their side.

## The deeper mechanism

Tribal Alignment has its own file — see `girard_shared_enemy_mechanism` — because the ethics of it are load-bearing. Short version: this only works when the scapegoat is real. Manufactured enemies break the mechanism and become manipulation.

## Why this connects to the framework corpus

Tribal Alignment is **scope discipline made interpersonal.** Scope discipline says: under-claim is discipline, over-claim is counterfeit. Applied to shared-enemy work: the standard you're both rejecting has to actually be a standard you genuinely reject. The moment you manufacture it to manipulate, scope collapses and the whole move becomes counterfeit. The framework's anti-hallucination posture shows up here as anti-manufactured-alliance posture.
